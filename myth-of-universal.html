The Myth of the Universal

There is no such thing as an "Average Person." When we design for the universal, we design for no one.

Architecture has a long history of obsessing over the "Standard Human." Le Corbusier created the Modulor, a system of proportions based on a 6-foot-tall British man. He believed that if he designed for this ideal, he designed for everyone.

In Making Architecture Through Being Human, we dismantle this myth. The "Universal Human" excludes women, the elderly, children, and anyone with different physical abilities.

"Standardization is an act of erasure. By smoothing out the differences between bodies, we turn architecture into a tool of exclusion."

1. The Digital Modulor

In the 21st century, the Modulor has returned, but it is made of data.

As explored in The Hidden Lives of Algorithms, digital platforms rely on the "Data Double"—a statistical profile of who they think the user is.

Figure 1: The Trap of the Bell Curve. Optimizing for the center creates hostility at the margins.

Algorithms are trained on large datasets. They are excellent at serving the "Average" behavior. But if you are an outlier—if your data doesn't fit the curve—the system breaks.

  • Facial Recognition: Fails to see darker skin tones because the "Universal" training set was white.
  • Smart Cities: Optimize traffic for the "Commuter" (Source-Goal), ignoring the "Wanderer" or the child playing.

2. Specificity is Rigor

The counter-position to the Universal is Specificity.

We do not design for "People"; we design for specific bodies in specific contexts. Embodied cognition is not generic—it is situated.

A rigorous designer asks: "Whose body is this geometry privileging?" If the answer is "Everyone," you haven't looked closely enough.
